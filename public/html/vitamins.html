Vitamins

Vitamins and minerals are micronutrients required by the body to carry out a range of normal functions. However, these micronutrients are not produced in our bodies and must be derived from the food we eat.

Vitamins are organic substances classified as either fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) or water-soluble (C and B-complex). Fat-soluble vitamins dissolve in fat and can accumulate in the body. Water-soluble vitamins must dissolve in water to be absorbed and are excreted if unused.

Minerals, like calcium, sodium, and zinc, are inorganic and needed in small amounts. They're absorbed from food or water, often via plants or animals.

The U.S. National Academy of Medicine defines DRIs (Dietary Reference Intakes), which are used as nutritional guidelines for different life stages.

What About Multivitamins?

A balanced diet with fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and healthy fats should provide necessary nutrients. But multivitamins can help fill nutritional gaps when diet falls short.

Before the 1930s, vitamins came only from food. Supplements became common later, and governments began fortifying foods with nutrients like iodine and folic acid to prevent common deficiencies.
